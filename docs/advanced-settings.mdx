:::tip
The advanced settings menu provides configuration options that let you customize how we process and analyze your data.
:::

Access advanced settings by tapping the **Advanced Settings** button on the neutral pose screen.

![Advanced Settings button location](/img/docs/advanced-settings/Doc1.png)

## Scaling Setup

We use data from the neutral pose to scale the musculoskeletal model to the subject's anthropometry.

**By default**, we assume the subject stands upright with feet pointing forward — straight back and no bending or rotation at the hips, knees, or ankles.

**If the subject cannot adopt this pose**, select the **"Any pose"** scaling setup. This does not assume a specific posture but still requires all body segments to be visible by at least two cameras.

We recommend using the default scaling setup unless the subject cannot stand in an upright neutral pose.

## Frame Rate

You can record videos at **60, 120, or 240 Hz**. The default is **120 Hz**.

- Higher frame rates may improve accuracy for dynamic tasks like running, but increase processing time.
- For most cases, **120 Hz is recommended**.
- If processing speed is a concern, **60 Hz** reduces computation time — consider applying a filter (see below) to mitigate the increased noise.

:::warning
The frame rate applies to **all motion trials** in your session.
:::

## Musculoskeletal Model

We support two models:

- **Full body model** — a 33 degree-of-freedom musculoskeletal model from [Lai et al. 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28900782/) with modified hip abductor muscle paths from [Uhlrich et al. 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35798755/).
- **Full body model with ISB shoulder** *(default)* — same as above, but with a 6 degree-of-freedom shoulder complex joint incorporating a scapulothoracic body with 3 translational degrees of freedom relative to the torso. The glenohumeral joint uses the Y-X-Y rotation sequence [recommended by the ISB](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15844264/). Recommended for upper extremity tasks (e.g., throwing), but suitable for all tasks.

## Marker Augmenter Model

We use an LSTM model to predict the 3D position of 43 anatomical markers from 20 video keypoints. These markers are used as input to OpenSim for inverse kinematics.

- **v0.3** *(default)* — more accurate and robust across different activities, inspired by [this paper](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2024.07.13.603382v1). Recommended for most use cases.
- **v0.2** — may produce better results specifically for walking and running, as it was trained more heavily on gait data.

## Filter Frequency

We apply a low-pass Butterworth filter to smooth 2D video keypoints.

**Default behavior:**
- Half the frame rate (e.g., 30 Hz at 60 Hz recording), except for gait activities which default to **12 Hz**.

You can enter a custom filter frequency here.

:::warning
- This filter frequency applies to **all motion trials** in your session.
- Per the Nyquist theorem, the filter frequency must be less than half the frame rate. If you enter a higher value, we will cap it at half the frame rate.
- Consult the literature for a suitable filter frequency for your specific task. If unsure, use the default.
:::
